# Core Vocabulary

## The Email Phrase Taxonomy

Corporate email phrases are organized into four functions. Mix freely. Meaning is optional.

---

## Family 1: The Openers

Every email begins with one of these. They mean nothing. They are mandatory.

| Phrase | When to Use |
|---|---|
| "Hope this finds you well." | Always. Even when you know it won't. |
| "Hope you had a great weekend." | Monday only. Do not use on Mondays in January. |
| "Thanks for your patience." | When you are 2 weeks late |
| "Following up on my previous email." | They read it. They ignored it. |
| "Apologies for the delayed response." | You forgot. This resets the clock. |
| "Just wanted to reach out." | You are about to ask for something. |
| "As discussed." | Nothing was discussed. This creates a paper trail. |

---

## Family 2: The Hedges

Attach to any sentence to remove all commitment from it.

| Hedge | Effect |
|---|---|
| "...if that makes sense." | Makes you sound collaborative, not uncertain |
| "...just thinking out loud." | Not accountable for anything that follows |
| "...happy to be corrected on this." | You will not be corrected |
| "...at your convenience." | Please do this immediately |
| "...no rush, but..." | There is a rush |
| "...feel free to..." | You must |
| "...whenever you get a chance." | I needed this yesterday |

---

## Family 3: The Redirects

Used to move ownership of a problem to another human.

| Phrase | Translation |
|---|---|
| "I want to make sure the right person owns this." | Not me |
| "Might be worth looping in [team]." | That team now owns it |
| "I'll defer to [name] on this one." | [Name] is now responsible |
| "This might be better suited for [other meeting]." | Not my problem until then |
| "Can we take this to the [committee/forum/sync]?" | Delay of 2-4 weeks minimum |
| "I'd love [name]'s input before we move forward." | [Name] will be unavailable |

---

## Family 4: The Closers

The email closer signals your emotional state without revealing it.

| Closer | True Meaning |
|---|---|
| "Best," | Neutral. Default. Nothing. |
| "Thanks," | I need something from you |
| "Thanks!" | I really need something from you |
| "Regards," | I am mildly annoyed |
| "Warm regards," | I am very annoyed |
| "Kindly," | I am British or furious |
| "Cheers," | I want to seem casual and approachable |
| "Talk soon," | We will not talk soon |
| "Looking forward to connecting," | I dread this |

---

## The Subject Line

The subject line is the most important part of the email. It must:

1. Sound urgent without creating urgency
2. Reference a meeting or document that may not exist
3. Not reveal the actual content

**Subject Line Formulas:**

```
Following up — [Vague Noun]
Re: Re: Re: [Original topic from 3 weeks ago]
Quick question re: [40-minute topic]
[ACTION REQUIRED] [Thing that doesn't require action]
FYI — [Thing you are being held responsible for]
Intro: [Person A] <> [Person B]    ← dumping two strangers on each other
[Name] / [Name] — [Vague Noun] sync    ← a meeting that is now your problem
```

## The CC Field — A Weapon Guide

The CC field is not for information sharing. It is a social instrument.

| CC Action | Message Sent |
|---|---|
| CC their manager | "I have documentation now." |
| CC your manager | "I am protected." |
| CC both managers | "This is an incident." |
| CC a distribution list | "Everyone knows." |
| Remove someone from CC | "You are no longer relevant to this conversation." |
| Add someone mid-thread | "Witness." |
| Reply-all when only one person needed it | Power move. No justification needed. |
